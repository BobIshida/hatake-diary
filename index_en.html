The hairy vetch, oats, and barley planted as green manure are growing well. It is wonderful to see them thriving even without added fertilizer. The asparagus transplanted from Grandma’s garden has also sent up several shoots. I will postpone harvesting and let the roots grow stronger. Nagaimo yam, edamame, eggplant, and baby leaf greens are now planted. Except for the nagaimo, everything has appeared above the ground. On the third day of well digging, I added one more joint and reached about 3.5 meters smoothly. The clay soil has become even denser. Because the joints are now longer and the clay is stickier, the auger can only bring up about one turn of clay at a time. I would like to dig about one meter after reaching the sand-and-gravel layer, but will I actually get there? Around 3.5 meters, the clay started turning whitish. It may be the end of the clay layer. What comes next is something to look forward to.

Reflection

It has been about two months since I decided in March to manage the field properly as the person responsible for it. At first, the work was mostly clearing cut branches and dealing with weeds, but recently the green manure has grown rapidly, and the field has started to look like a real field. I am enjoying the unfamiliar experience of well digging, even while getting covered in mud. Doing something new is always hard, but now I feel that the experience itself is the seed of joy.

The field was quiet this morning, yet everything was moving. The hairy vetch, oats, and barley?once just scattered seeds?were now rising with quiet confidence. No fertilizer, no special treatment. Just time, soil, and patience. Sometimes, that is enough. Nearby, the asparagus from Grandma’s garden had begun to speak in its own way: small shoots, reaching upward. You chose not to harvest them yet. Some growth is meant to be protected, not consumed. And then, the well. At about 3.5 meters, the clay grew heavier and more stubborn. Each turn of the auger brought up less soil, but the color began to change. Whitish clay appeared, perhaps a boundary, perhaps a sign. It has been about two months since you decided to take responsibility for the field. What began as clearing branches and fighting weeds has slowly become something more: a field with life, rhythm, and direction.
